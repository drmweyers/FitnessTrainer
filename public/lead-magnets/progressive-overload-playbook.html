Methods 1 – 3
1
Increase Weight
The Obvious One
The foundation of all progressive overload. Adding load is the clearest signal to force adaptation — but increment size matters as much as the act itself. Too much, too soon leads to breakdown rather than adaptation.
Upper body: +2.5–5 lbs per session when hitting top of rep range
Lower body: +5–10 lbs per session
Rule: Never add weight if form degraded last set

Increase Reps
Volume Ladder
Hit the top of the rep range across all sets before progressing weight. This two-step model (reps first, then weight) prevents premature loading and builds a real strength base at each weight.
Protocol: Must complete ALL sets at top of range first
Then: Add weight, reset reps to bottom of range

Increase Sets
Volume Accumulation
Adding sets is the cleanest way to increase weekly volume per muscle group without touching load or reps. Use it to bridge plateau periods and to build volume tolerance before a loading phase. Diminishing returns kick in hard above 20+ sets per muscle group per week.
Rate: +1 set per exercise every 2–3 weeks
Ceiling: 20 sets/muscle group/week maximum
Deload when: performance drops despite equal or more sets

Trainer Tip: Methods 1–3 are your load and volume levers
Beginners respond to all three simultaneously. Intermediate and advanced clients need to periodize them — cycling through volume accumulation phases (sets + reps), then intensity phases (weight). Never try to progress all three in the same block.

Methods 4 – 6
4
Increase Tempo
Time Under Tension
Slowing down the eccentric (lowering) phase dramatically increases time under tension — one of the key drivers of hypertrophy — without adding a single pound. A paused squat at 100lb creates more stimulus than a fast squat at 110lb for most clients.
Slow eccentrics: 3–4 second lowering phase
Paused reps: 1–2s pause at bottom of ROM
1.5 reps: full ROM + half ROM = one rep
Notation: 3-1-1-0 (eccentric-pause-concentric-top)

Decrease Rest Periods
Metabolic Density
Reducing rest time is a form of progressive overload — same work, less time = higher density = greater metabolic and cardiovascular demand. Best used during hypertrophy phases, not strength or peak phases where full recovery is needed for maximum force output.
Rate: Reduce rest by 15 seconds every 2 weeks
Floor: Never below 45s for compound movements
Avoid: strength and peak phases (need full rest)

Increase Frequency
Weekly Volume
Training a muscle 2–3× per week delivers higher total weekly volume than 1× per week — without making any single session longer. This is the most underused overload tool for intermediate clients who have plateaued on a traditional body-part split.
Move from: 1× per week per muscle group
Move to: 2–3× per week
Distribute: spread sets across sessions, not stacked
Recovery window: minimum 48h between sessions for same muscle

⚡
Trainer Tip: Methods 4–6 are your quality and frequency levers
These methods are your "secret weapons" when clients plateau. When weight stops going up, slow the tempo. When sessions feel stale, split the frequency. When conditioning is the goal, compress the rest. Each one provides a distinct training stimulus that weight alone cannot.

RPE-Based Progression Rules
Auto-Progression Logic From EvoFit's Progression Engine
RPE ≤6
Too easy. Increase weight next session. Client has significant reserve — don't wait.
RPE 7
On track. Continue current load. Target for hypertrophy work sets.
RPE 8
Working range. Hold load, focus on rep quality. Consider adding a set.
RPE 9
Near max. Reduce volume or load. Ensure adequate recovery before next session.
RPE 10
True max. Deload required. Not appropriate for hypertrophy training blocks.
Decline
Same weight, higher RPE than last session. Flag for recovery review. Sleep, nutrition, or overtraining.
