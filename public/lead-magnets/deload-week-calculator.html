Recovery is Programming
Know Exactly When & How to Deload
A deload week is a planned, strategic reduction in training volume and/or intensity. It's not weakness — it's the mechanism that allows your clients to absorb training stress and come back stronger.
The Science
What Happens During a Deload

Training creates accumulated fatigue that masks your true fitness level. A deload reduces systemic fatigue while maintaining training adaptations — allowing the fitness gains you've built to express themselves as actual performance.

Deload ≠ Rest Week

During a proper deload you still train — typically 4–6 sessions. You reduce volume (sets × reps) by 40–60%, keep intensity moderate, and maintain movement patterns. Stopping entirely causes rapid detraining and wastes your training block.

General Rule
3–6WEEKS
Deload every 3–6 weeks of hard training

Beginners recover faster and can push to 6 weeks. Advanced trainees accumulate fatigue faster and may need to deload every 3–4 weeks. Listen to both data and athlete feedback when scheduling deloads.

Warning Signs — Schedule a Deload Immediately
📉
Stalled Progress
No strength or performance gains for 2+ consecutive sessions despite correct programming
😴
Persistent Fatigue
Client reports heavy legs, sluggish warm-ups, or can't hit target loads that felt easy recently
🧠
Motivation Drop
Dreading sessions, skipping workouts, or lost enthusiasm for training they normally enjoy
🦵
Joint Discomfort
Achy knees, sore elbows, or nagging shoulder pain that wasn't there 2–3 weeks ago
Deload Protocol by Training Phase
Training Phase Deload Frequency Volume Reduction Intensity Guidance Primary Goal
Hypertrophy Every 4 Weeks 40–50% reduction Keep intensity (same loads, fewer sets) Restore tissue recovery
Strength Every 3–4 Weeks 50% reduction Reduce to ~70% 1RM; maintain patterns CNS recovery, joint health
Power / Athletic Every 3 Weeks 60% reduction Reduce to ~60% intensity; cut explosives Neural fatigue management
