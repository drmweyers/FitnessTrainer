Introduction

Why Most Trainers Lose Clients After 3 Months

EvoFit
The 90-Day Dropout Problem

Research consistently shows that over 60% of personal training clients quit within their first three months. The reason is almost never the trainer's personality or gym quality. It's the program. Clients plateau, get confused, or feel like they're just doing random workouts with no clear direction — and they leave.

The Core Problem

A random collection of exercises is not a program. A program is a purposeful, periodised sequence of training stimuli designed to produce a specific physiological adaptation over time. Without periodisation, clients stop progressing. Without progression, they stop paying.

Program vs. Random Exercises

A real program manipulates load, volume, intensity, and rest in a calculated sequence. Each session builds on the last.

The Progression Equation

Systematic overload + appropriate recovery = sustainable adaptation. Miss either side and results stall permanently.

Chapter 1

The 6-Phase Periodisation Framework

EvoFit

Periodisation is the systematic planning of athletic training. It involves progressive cycling of various aspects of a training program during a specific period. Here are the six phases every complete annual plan should include:

1

Anatomical Adaptation

2–4 Weeks

Prepares tendons, ligaments, and stabiliser muscles for heavier loading ahead. High reps (15–20), light loads, full body movements. This phase is often skipped by impatient trainers — and later regretted. Injury prevention starts here.

2

Hypertrophy

6–12 Weeks

The foundation of physique development. Moderate loads (67–85% 1RM), moderate-to-high volume (3–5 sets of 8–15 reps). Metabolic stress and mechanical tension drive muscle growth. This is where most client transformations are built.

3

Strength

4–8 Weeks

Builds maximal force production by recruiting more motor units. Heavy loads (85–95% 1RM), lower reps (3–6), longer rest periods (3–5 min). Neural adaptations take priority over muscle growth in this phase.

4

Power

2–4 Weeks

Converts strength gains into explosive force. Submaximal loads (30–70% 1RM) moved as fast as possible. Olympic lifts, plyometrics, medicine ball throws. Primarily for athletes, but adds variety and athleticism for general clients.

5

Peaking

1–3 Weeks

Maximises performance for a specific event, competition, or test. Volume drops significantly (40–60%), intensity stays high. Allows full recovery while maintaining strength. Used before competitions, photoshoots, or milestone assessments.

6

Active Recovery

1–2 Weeks

Structured rest that allows physiological and psychological restoration. Light movement, mobility, low-intensity cardio. Supercompensation (the rebound effect) occurs here — skip it and you leave gains on the table.

Chapter 2

ACWR Training Load Monitoring

EvoFit

The Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio (ACWR) is the gold-standard method for monitoring whether your client is training at a productive level or drifting into overtraining territory. Originally developed in elite sport science, it's now accessible to every trainer through modern coaching software.

The ACWR Formula
Acute
7-day training load
÷
Chronic
28-day training load
=
ACWR Score
Target: 0.8 – 1.3

Training load is calculated as session RPE × duration (minutes). For example, a client who rates a 60-minute session at RPE 7 generates a load score of 420 arbitrary units (AU). Track this daily, and the rolling average tells you exactly where your client sits on the readiness spectrum.

< 0.8
Undertraining

Load too low. Client is deconditioned, losing fitness. Increase training stimulus.

0.8 – 1.3
Sweet Spot

Optimal training zone. Client is adapting safely. Maintain or incrementally progress.

> 1.5
Danger Zone

Injury risk 2–4× higher. Load increased too fast. Immediate volume reduction required.

⚠️

The Spike Warning: A single hard week after low training volume is one of the highest injury predictors. Never increase weekly load by more than 10% and always ensure your 28-day chronic load base supports the acute demand. "Doing too much too soon" is responsible for the majority of non-contact training injuries.

RPE vs Percentage-Based Loading

EvoFit

The debate between autoregulated RPE loading and fixed percentage-based programming is one of the most practical decisions you'll make for each client. The truth? Neither is universally superior — context determines which wins.

Use RPE When:

  • Client is intermediate or advanced (self-aware)
  • Daily readiness fluctuates (poor sleep, stress)
  • No recent 1RM test data is available
  • Autoregulation fits client psychology
  • Injury rehab requires daily load modulation

Use Percentages When:

  • Client is a beginner (no reliable RPE gauge)
  • Peaking for a tested competition
  • You have recent, reliable 1RM data
  • Client prefers structure over intuition
  • Accessory lifts with fixed progression targets

The Blended Approach (Recommended for Most Clients)

Use RPE for main compound lifts (squat, deadlift, bench, press, row) where daily readiness significantly impacts safe loading. Use percentage-based loading for accessories where consistency matters more than autoregulation. EvoFit Trainer's progression engine supports both simultaneously — assign RPE targets to primary movements and fixed percentages to secondary work, all within the same program block.

The 80/20 Rule of Exercise Selection

80% of your clients' results come from 20% of their exercises. Compound, multi-joint movements — squats, deadlifts, pressing, pulling — drive the overwhelming majority of strength, hypertrophy, and body composition changes. Fill primary slots with these first, then use accessories to address weak points, add volume, or improve aesthetics.

Home Gym Programming Minimal Kit

  • Dumbbells: Romanian DL, DB Press, DB Row, Lunges
  • Resistance bands: Face pulls, lateral raises, rows
  • Bodyweight: Push-ups, pull-ups, dips, single-leg work
  • Focus on unilateral movements to address imbalances

Commercial Gym Programming Full Kit

  • Barbell compounds as primary movements every session
  • Cable machines for constant tension on accessories
  • Machines for isolated hypertrophy (leg extension, etc.)

Chapter 6

Client Assessment Checklist

EvoFit

A thorough initial assessment is the difference between a generic program and a life-changing one. Use this checklist before building any client's first program. This data feeds directly into EvoFit Trainer's client profile, enabling personalised programming from day one.

1

PAR-Q Health Screening

  • Has a doctor ever told them they have a heart condition?
  • Do they feel chest pain during physical activity?
  • Have they experienced dizziness or loss of consciousness?
  • Any bone or joint problems aggravated by exercise?
  • Currently on any medication affecting exercise safety?
  • Any other condition requiring medical supervision?
2

Movement Screen Basics

  • Overhead squat assessment (hip mobility, ankle dorsiflexion)
  • Single-leg balance test (stability, proprioception)
  • Hip hinge pattern (deadlift mechanics, hamstring flexibility)
  • Thoracic spine rotation (overhead press capability)
  • Push pattern (scapular control, anterior shoulder)
  • Pull pattern (lat engagement, elbow path)
3

Goal-Setting Framework

  • Primary goal: strength / hypertrophy / performance / general fitness
  • Timeline: when do they need to achieve the goal?
  • Training history: years, frequency, injuries
  • Available training days per week (3, 4, 5, or 6?)
  • Session duration available (45, 60, 90 min?)
  • Equipment access: home gym, commercial, outdoor?
4

Baseline Performance Metrics

  • Max push-ups (unbroken, bodyweight baseline)
  • 1RM or estimated 1RM on primary compound lifts
  • Bodyweight measurements (if relevant to goals)
  • Resting heart rate and HRV if available
  • Prior injury history documented and flagged
  • Lifestyle factors: sleep quality, stress levels
